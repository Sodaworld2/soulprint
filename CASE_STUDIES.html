Case Study 1

The Sculptor: Protecting a 3D Mesh

A sculptor creates physical works and wants to sell authorised 3D meshes for people to print, display in virtual worlds, or use in games — but only with a valid licence. Anyone using an unauthorised copy is caught.

🧑‍🎨

Thandiwe Mkhize

SCULPTOR · JOHANNESBURG · BRONZE + DIGITAL

Thandiwe creates bronze sculptures inspired by Zulu mythology. She's been approached by a metaverse platform wanting to sell 3D scans of her work. She's seen other sculptors' work ripped from Instagram and turned into 3D prints sold on Etsy without permission. She wants to sell authorised digital twins — but needs proof of ownership and a way to catch pirates.

1

Capture: High-Resolution 3D Scan

Thandiwe takes her bronze sculpture to a photogrammetry studio. 200+ photographs from every angle are processed into a high-resolution 3D mesh (.OBJ / .GLB / .USDZ). She also photographs the physical sculpture under controlled lighting for the 2D registration.

PHOTOGRAMMETRY 200+ IMAGES .OBJ / .GLB
2

Upload to Soulprint Forensic Lab

Thandiwe uploads both the 3D mesh file AND the reference photographs to Soulprint. She selects the "Forensic Lab" tier ($149) because she needs maximum protection for commercial licensing.

📁 What she uploads:
  • 3D Mesh: .GLB file (12.4 MB) — the complete digital twin of the sculpture
  • Reference Photos: 8 high-res photographs of the physical bronze under controlled lighting
  • Process Documentation: Video of the sculpting process (clay maquette to bronze casting)
  • Creator Statement: Cultural context, technique description, materials used
3

Layer 1: Creation Biometrics — Process Verification

Soulprint analyses the creation documentation. The sculpting video is processed frame-by-frame to extract a timeline of the creation process — from initial clay sketch through maquette to final bronze.

🧬 What's extracted:
  • Creation timeline: 47 distinct stages identified across 6 hours of video
  • Material transitions: Clay → wax → ceramic shell → bronze detected and timestamped
  • Tool marks analysis: Characteristic sculpting tool patterns identified in close-up frames
  • Artist hand presence: Confirmed human hands visible throughout — not a 3D print or CNC
VIDEO FRAME ANALYSIS TEMPORAL MAPPING TOOL MARK ID
4

Layer 2: Content DNA — 3D Mesh Fingerprint

The 3D mesh is mathematically fingerprinted using techniques adapted from computational geometry.

📊 3D-specific analysis:
  • Vertex distribution hash: Statistical fingerprint of the mesh's 487,000 vertices — their spatial distribution is unique like a fingerprint
  • Surface curvature map: Gaussian and mean curvature computed at every vertex. The curvature signature is unique to this specific sculpture
  • Topology hash: The mesh's connectivity (which vertices connect to which) is hashed — survives scaling, rotation, and translation
  • Bounding box ratios: Height:width:depth proportions captured as a quick-match identifier
  • Surface area / volume ratio: Mathematical constant for this specific form
🖼️ 2D reference photo analysis (per photograph):
  • Colour DNA: RGB/HSV/LAB channel separation — the patina colour signature of the bronze
  • Perceptual hash: pHash + dHash for each angle — finds cropped/resized copies online
  • Regional entropy: 8×8 grid Shannon entropy map — complexity fingerprint of the surface texture
  • Edge orientation: Sobel gradient histograms capture the sculpture's silhouette signature from each angle
  • Statistical moments: Mean, variance, skewness, kurtosis per channel per region
VERTEX HASH GAUSSIAN CURVATURE TOPOLOGY pHASH ENTROPY MAP SOBEL
5

Layer 3: Deep Forensic Analysis

Server-side analysis runs the heavy computation on both the reference photos and the 3D mesh data.

🔬 What runs:
  • AI generation check (FIRE): Frequency-guided reconstruction error test on all 8 reference photos. Result: PASS — no AI generation artifacts detected. These are photographs of a real physical object.
  • FFT spectral analysis: Power spectrum of each photo checked for GAN/diffusion upsampling artifacts. Result: CLEAN — natural frequency distribution consistent with camera capture.
  • EXIF validation: Camera: Nikon Z7 II, lens: 24-70mm f/2.8, GPS: Johannesburg. Metadata consistent and internally coherent.
  • PRNU camera fingerprint: Sensor noise pattern extracted. All 8 photos confirmed from the same individual camera unit.
  • 3D mesh integrity: No signs of automated mesh generation (AI-generated meshes have characteristic vertex clustering patterns). Vertex distribution is organic — consistent with photogrammetry from a physical object.
  • ELA on photos: Uniform error levels — no regions pasted or composited.
FIRE (CVPR 2025) FFT PRNU ELA EXIF MESH INTEGRITY

Soulprint searches for similar existing works across its registry and the open web.

  • CLIP semantic search: 3 visually similar works found (other Zulu mythology sculptures) — all by different artists, different forms. Similarity: 34%, 29%, 22%. Well below the 85% alert threshold.
  • Reverse image search: Thandiwe's Instagram posts found with the same sculpture. Confirmed as her public portfolio. No unauthorised copies detected yet.
  • 3D model databases: No matching meshes found on Sketchfab, TurboSquid, or CGTrader.

Layer 5: Invisible Watermark Injection

The certificate is embedded directly into the assets — both the 3D mesh and the reference images.

🔐 What's injected:
  • 3D mesh watermark: Certificate ID (SP-TM8K4X2A) embedded by subtly perturbing vertex positions in the least-significant decimal places. Invisible to the eye, survives format conversion (.OBJ → .FBX → .GLB), detectable by Soulprint decoder.
  • Photo watermarks: Spread-spectrum + QIM dual-layer watermark in all 8 reference photos. Certificate ID woven into the pixel data. Survives JPEG, scaling, cropping, screenshots.
  • Licence payload: The watermark doesn't just contain the certificate ID — it encodes the licence type (commercial, limited edition, personal), the authorised holder, and an expiry timestamp.

The Painter: Protecting an Original Canvas

A painter creates original canvases and sells them through galleries. She needs to prove her work is human-made, establish a forensic record before sale, and catch anyone who uses images of her paintings without licence.

🎨

Amara Okafor

OIL PAINTER · CAPE TOWN · LARGE-FORMAT CANVASES

Amara paints large-format oil canvases — vibrant landscapes with thick impasto technique. Her gallery sells originals for R80K-R250K. She's discovered AI models trained on her Instagram photos can now generate "Amara-style" paintings. Collectors are asking: "How do I know this is really yours, not AI?" She needs proof.

1

Capture: Painting the Work with Soulprint Recording

Amara installs the Soulprint plugin for Procreate on her iPad, where she sketches compositions before painting on canvas. The plugin silently records her creation process as she works on the initial sketch.

✍️ What the plugin captures during sketching:
  • Pressure curves: 4,200 strokes recorded with full pressure data (0.0–1.0 range) per sample at 120Hz

  • Jerk (3rd derivative): The rate of acceleration change — captures involuntary hand tremor patterns unique to Amara
  • Tilt + azimuth: Apple Pencil tilt data showing her habitual 35-degree working angle
  • Undo/redo log: 47 undos, 12 redos — the pattern of revision that AI cannot produce
  • Temporal heatmap: She starts top-left, works in spirals — a habitual working pattern building her biometric profile

When the canvas painting is complete, she photographs it under controlled lighting using her Nikon Z7 II with a colour reference card.

The Printmaker: Protecting a Limited Edition

An illustrator creates a digital artwork and sells it as a limited edition of 50 signed prints. Each print needs to be individually authenticated, numbered, and traceable. Unauthorised reproductions must be detectable.

🖨️

Kenji Matsuda

DIGITAL ILLUSTRATOR · TOKYO/ONLINE · LIMITED EDITIONS
